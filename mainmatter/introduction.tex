\section{Background and Motivation}
\label{sec:background_motivation}

The Dutch housing market faces a structural shortage of roughly 400,000 homes, about 4.8\% of the national housing stock. At the government's target of 100,000 new dwellings per year, closing this gap would take at least four years of construction at full capacity \cite{ABF2025, VRO2025}. Underlying this deficit are profound demographic and structural shifts that expand far beyond standard supply constraints. Foremost among these drivers is continuous net population growth paired with a significant, long-term societal transition toward smaller household sizes~\cite{ABF2025, ABN2025}.\\\newline
Crucially, economic research by ABN AMRO indicates that the deepening housing crisis is driven more by internal demographic shifts than by simple population growth~\cite{ABN2025}. This creates a profound analytical paradox that purely quantitative trend models fail to reconcile. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:abn_demographics}, the total number of housing units per inhabitant in the Netherlands has actually risen consistently over the last century, reaching an all-time high of approximately 0.46 homes per person. On paper, a baseline data-driven trend extrapolation would suggest that housing availability per capita is better than ever. However, the real-world reality is a shortage because the average household size is rapidly shrinking as a massive share of the population transitions to single-person households. Because more people are living alone, the country structurally requires far more individual dwellings to house the exact same number of citizens~\cite{ABN2025}. Purely volume-based historical data treats a citizen from 1950 and a citizen from 2026 identically, completely ignoring this qualitative demographic mismatch.\\\newline
But hitting the necessary building targets to correct this imbalance remains highly unlikely. Project feasibility is under pressure from rising mortgage rates, supply-chain disruptions, and climbing material and labor costs~\cite{ABF2025}. On top of this, the Netherlands operates under a heavy administrative burden: dense paperwork and slow permitting procedures routinely delay project initialization, with average timelines stretching between 8 to 12 years from initial conceptualization to formal key handover~\cite{VRO2025, ABN2025}. Furthermore, increasingly strict environmental boundaries, particularly the persistent nitrogen crisis, combined with severe personnel shortages have frozen or stalled major residential projects nationwide~\cite{VRO2025, EIB2025}.

\section{The Data Challenge}
\label{sec:data_challenge}

This complex climate makes the future hard to model from historical data alone, since simple trend extrapolation cannot capture these multi-faceted disruptions. Unregistered labor migrants are a prime example of this embedded data deficiency on the demand side. Official housing statistics rely heavily on municipal registries (\textit{Basisregistratie Personen}), which completely fail to capture this group, even though they actively occupy the residential housing stock. As a result, official figures systematically understate true demand, and the real shortage is likely worse than reported~\cite{ABF2025}.

A second critical example lies on the supply side: the unprecedented labor scarcity bottlenecking construction capacity. While official government targets aim for 100,000 new homes annually, traditional forecasting models assume a relatively stable labor pool. However, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:vacaturegraad}, the construction sector  faces a historic vacancy rate of 80 unfilled roles per 1,000 jobs as of mid-2025, nearly double the national economic average of 44~\cite{EIB2025}. Purely data-driven extrapolations are structurally blind to these physical labor constraints, leading to highly optimistic production estimates that cannot be realized in practice.

These instances represent a broader issue: many highly relevant market variables are simply not captured in empirical data. Purely data-driven models cannot account for them, but domain experts often can, drawing on contextual and experiential knowledge that doesn't show up in registries. This is why expert elicitation is a necessary complement to data-driven forecasting for the 2027 housing outlook, not a replacement for it, but a mathematically sound way to capture what empirical databases structurally miss.
\section{Structured Expert Judgment and the Classical Model}
\label{sec:sej_classical_model}

However, relying on the unguided expertise of individuals can be challenging, as human judgment is systematically vulnerable to cognitive heuristics and psychological biases, most notably overconfidence and anchoring. To achieve scientific validity and mitigate these flaws, expert judgment experiments must be highly structured and strictly follow the general principles of rational consensus: reproducibility, accountability, empirical control, neutrality, and fairness.

To satisfy these programmatic principles, this study employs the Classical Model for Structured Expert Judgment (SEJ), developed by Roger M. Cooke. The Classical Model operates as a performance-based linear pooling framework. Rather than treating all expert assessments equally, the model imposes empirical control by scoring experts on a series of calibration (or ``seed'') variables from their field where the true values are already known. Experts are subsequently weighted according to a mathematical combination of their statistical accuracy (calibration) and their informativeness, creating a scoring rule that heavily incentivizes them to state their true, unbiased beliefs.

\section{Objective and Report Structure}
\label{sec:objective_structure}

The primary objective of this research is to apply the Classical Model to construct a performance-weighted probabilistic forecast of the Dutch residential housing market for the year 2027. By eliciting the 5\%, 50\%, and 95\% quantiles from a panel of domain experts, this study aims to mathematically combine their assessments into an optimized ``Decision Maker'' forecast that formally captures the epistemic uncertainty where historical empirical data is structurally blind.

The remainder of this report is structured as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Chapter 2} introduces the theory and mathematical foundation behind the Classical Model.
    \item \textbf{Chapter 3} details the elicitation process, outlining the panel of experts, the specific target questions of interest, and the rationale behind the chosen calibration questions.
    \item \textbf{Chapter 4} provides a performance analysis of the experts using the \textit{Excalibur} software environment.
    \item \textbf{Chapter 5} presents the optimized forecasts for the questions of interest.
    \item \textbf{Chapter 6} offers a synthesis of conclusions and a discussion of the study's limitations.
\end{itemize}
