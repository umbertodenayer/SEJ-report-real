\section{Programmatic Implementation (The Anduril Approach)}
\label{sec:programmatic_implementation}

The Classical Model was implemented as a transparent Python pipeline rather than evaluated through the legacy Windows-only Excalibur binary. This ``Anduril approach'' expresses each mathematical step directly in inspectable source code. NumPy provides numerical arrays and weighted operations; SciPy supplies Brent's root-finding algorithm through \texttt{scipy.optimize.brentq}; pandas supports tabular preparation of the Formspree export; and Matplotlib produces the diagnostic figures. The submitted aggregation module is reproduced in Appendix~\ref{appendix:programmatic_code}.

For every target, the code constructs a piecewise uniform or log-uniform cumulative distribution for each expert over the intrinsic range. Linear pooling is then applied twice: once with equal weights and once with the optimized performance weights. Because the pooled distribution does not generally have a closed-form inverse, the 5th, 50th, and 95th percentiles are obtained by numerically solving $F_{\mathrm{DM}}(x)-p=0$ for $p\in\{0.05,0.50,0.95\}$. This procedure implements the same mathematical model described in Chapter~2 without relying on opaque software defaults.

Programmatic implementation strengthens two principles of rational consensus. \textit{Reproducibility} follows because another analyst can inspect the data transformations, weighting rule, and inversion procedure. \textit{Accountability} follows because assumptions such as the 10\% overshoot and the uniform/log-uniform choice are explicit, versionable, and open to sensitivity testing. The pipeline was executed against the seven anonymous expert assessments, and its printed quantiles were used directly in Chapter~5.

\section{Background Measures}
\label{sec:background_measures}

Relative information is measured against a minimally informative background distribution over an intrinsic range $[L,U]$. For each variable, the range is defined by the smallest and largest elicited bounds and, for calibration variables, the realization. The observed range is extended by 10\% at both ends. Uniform backgrounds are used for proportions and rates (Seeds 5, 7, and 9; Target 4); log-uniform backgrounds are used for the remaining positive scale variables. A log-uniform background assigns equal probability to equal ratios and is invariant to changes of measurement units.

Within the intrinsic range, each expert distribution is piecewise uniform or piecewise log-uniform over the four inter-quantile intervals, with probability masses $(0.05,0.45,0.45,0.05)$. The calculations were reproduced in Python using the same definitions as the Classical Model: bin counts determine calibration, relative entropy determines information, and numerical inversion of the pooled cumulative distribution produces the target quantiles. A sensitivity check using 5\% and 20\% overshoot and an all-uniform alternative left the optimal threshold unchanged and moved the dwelling median by fewer than 100 units.

\section{Decision Maker Comparison}
\label{sec:dm_comparison}

Three Decision Makers are evaluated on the seed variables. The Equal Weight Decision Maker (EWDM) assigns $1/7$ to every expert. The fixed-threshold PWDM assigns zero weight when $\operatorname{Cal}(e)<0.05$ and normalizes the remaining performance weights. The optimized PWDM selects $\alpha^*$ to maximize the product of the pooled distribution's calibration and mean information.

The EWDM has the highest calibration ($0.6828$) but is diffuse, yielding mean information of only $0.321$ and a combined score of $0.2191$. The fixed-threshold PWDM is sharper and improves the combined score to $0.2447$. The optimized PWDM uses $\alpha^*=\OptimalAlpha$, retains five experts, and achieves the largest combined score ($0.2988$). It is therefore selected for the target forecasts in Chapter~\ref{chapter:results}. The selection is based on the joint scoring rule rather than on calibration or informativeness alone.
