\section{Narrative Interpretation}
\label{sec:narrative_interpretation}

The optimized PWDM assigns positive weight only to Experts 1--5; Experts 6 and 7 receive zero because their calibration scores fall below the optimized threshold. The distinction is most visible for the housing-supply variables. For permits, the EWDM upper bound reaches 111,352 because it includes Expert 6's high assessment, whereas the PWDM upper bound is 88,840. Performance weighting therefore produces a substantially tighter and more informative pipeline forecast. The dwelling interval changes less dramatically because the individual assessments are more similar, although the PWDM still shifts the median upward from 72,792 to 74,847.

The completed-dwellings forecast is the principal policy result. The PWDM median is approximately 25\% below the government's ambition of 100,000 dwellings per year, and even its 95th percentile remains below that benchmark. Conditional on this panel and model specification, reaching 100,000 completed dwellings in 2027 is assigned little probability. The permit median of 67,915 likewise does not imply the acceleration required to resolve the shortage rapidly.

For Rotterdam, the optimized median transaction price is \euro669,298, with a 90\% interval from \euro587,397 to \euro763,297. Free-sector rent growth has a median of 4.96\% and a 90\% interval from 2.35\% to 7.53\%. These forecasts describe affordability pressure, but the pooled distributions do not identify the causal contribution of any single housing-market mechanism.
