\section{Housing Supply}

For completed dwellings, the optimized DM gives a median of \DwellingsMedian{} and a 90\% interval from \DwellingsFive{} to \DwellingsNinetyFive{}. The median is roughly 25\% below the government's annual ambition of 100,000 dwellings, and even the 95th percentile remains below that benchmark. Within the elicited model, failure to reach the target is therefore not merely the central expectation; it persists across almost the entire assessed uncertainty range.

The permit forecast is similarly restrained: the optimized median is \PermitsMedian{}, with a 90\% interval of \PermitsFive{}--\PermitsNinetyFive{}. Permits are a pipeline indicator rather than completed supply, so the two quantities should not be compared one-for-one. Nevertheless, their joint pattern supports the interpretation that the production pipeline is unlikely to accelerate sufficiently by 2027 to remove the structural shortage.

The EWDM medians for both supply variables are close to their optimized counterparts. Its upper permit bound is materially larger, however, because the two poorly calibrated experts supplied wider or higher assessments. Performance weighting therefore affects tail uncertainty more strongly than the central supply forecast.

\section{Prices and Rents}

For an existing owner-occupied dwelling in Amsterdam, the optimized median transaction price is \euro\PriceMedian, with a 90\% interval from \euro\PriceFive{} to \euro\PriceNinetyFive. The relatively broad interval reflects uncertainty about financing conditions, local supply, and persistent demand. The optimized and equal-weight medians differ by only about \euro2,000, indicating that the panel's central price assessment is robust to the weighting rule.

The optimized median forecast for annual free-sector rent growth is \RentMedian\%, bounded by \RentFive\% and \RentNinetyFive\%. The lower bound remains positive, indicating that every part of the pooled central 90\% distribution implies continued nominal rent growth. The result is consistent with sustained scarcity, but it should not be read as isolating the causal effect of any single policy or market mechanism.

\section{Robustness and Interpretation}

Across all four targets, optimized and equal-weight medians are similar. The greatest difference appears in the upper tail of building permits, where the EWDM reaches 111,352 compared with \PermitsNinetyFive{} for the optimized DM. This comparison is reassuring for the qualitative conclusions while also showing why performance weighting matters: central expectations are broadly shared, but poorly calibrated experts can materially widen tails.

Figure~\ref{fig:target_forecasts} makes the robustness comparison visible across different scales. The optimized intervals are generally narrower, while the median locations remain close to the equal-weight results.

The most decision-relevant conclusion is the gap between the dwelling forecast and the 100,000-unit policy ambition. The result does not prove that the target cannot be reached. It states that, conditional on this panel, elicitation instrument, and Classical Model specification, the target lies beyond the optimized 95th percentile. That distinction preserves the probabilistic meaning of the analysis.
